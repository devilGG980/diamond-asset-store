# Top 10 Best External Hard Drives for Video Editors

If you are a video editor, your data is your lifeblood. One hardware failure can mean losing weeks of work or years of memories. Furthermore, the *speed* of your hard drive directly affects your editing experience. If your drive is slow, your timeline will lag, no matter how fast your computer is.

In 2026, we have two main choices: **HDDs** (cheap, high capacity, slow) and **SSDs** (expensive, low capacity, lightning fast). Here are the top 10 best external hard drives for video editors today.

## 1. Samsung T7 Shield (The All-Rounder)
The T7 Shield is the gold standard for most creators. It’s an SSD with speeds up to 1,050 MB/s, which is more than enough for 4K editing.
*   **Why it’s great:** It’s rugged, water-resistant, and drop-resistant. It’s the perfect drive to throw in your bag and take to a shoot.

## 2. SanDisk Professional PRO-G40 (The Speed Demon)
If you are editing 8K video or working with massive ProRes files, the T7 won't be enough. The PRO-G40 uses Thunderbolt 3 to achieve speeds up to 3,000 MB/s.
*   **Why it’s great:** It is one of the fastest external drives on the planet. It feels as fast as your computer’s internal drive.

## 3. LaCie Rugged SSD Pro
The "Orange Drive" is a staple on professional film sets. The Pro version is a Thunderbolt-only drive built for extreme durability.
*   **Why it’s great:** It is nearly indestructible and features professional-grade cooling to prevent speed drops during long exports.

## 4. Crucial X10 Pro
This is the best value-for-performance SSD on the market. It offers 2,100 MB/s speeds in a device the size of a credit card.
*   **Why it’s great:** It’s incredibly small and light, making it the best choice for travel vloggers who need to keep their gear light.

## 5. WD My Passport (The Budget Backup)
This is an HDD, not an SSD. You shouldn't edit off this drive, but you should use it to *backup* your finished projects.
*   **Why it’s great:** You can get 5TB of storage for under $120. It’s the most cost-effective way to archive your work.

## 6. G-Technology G-RAID 2
This is a desktop-only drive designed for "Main" storage. It contains two high-speed HDDs working together in "RAID 0" to double their speed.
*   **Why it’s great:** It offers massive capacity (up to 40TB) while being fast enough for moderate 4K editing.

## 7. OWC Envoy Pro FX
This is a universal drive that works on Thunderbolt and standard USB-C. Most fast drives only work well on one or the other.
*   **Why it’s great:** Total compatibility. Whether you are plugging into an old PC or a new Mac, it will always give you the maximum possible speed.

## 8. Seagate One Touch Hub
If you have a lot of peripherals (cameras, mice, keyboards), this drive doubles as a USB hub.
*   **Why it’s great:** It cleans up your desk while providing up to 20TB of storage for your B-roll and archives.

## 9. Sabrent Rocket Nano
As the name suggests, this is one of the smallest SSDs in existence. 
*   **Why it’s great:** It’s about the size of a thumb drive but offers the performance of a professional external SSD.

## 10. The "DIY" NVMe Enclosure
If you want the best performance for the lowest price, buy an internal M.2 NVMe SSD and put it in a $30 USB-C enclosure.
*   **Why it’s great:** You can choose exactly which drive goes inside and upgrade it later as storage prices drop.

## Conclusion
Your hard drive is the foundation of your editing setup. Invest in a fast SSD (like the Samsung T7 or Crucial X10) for your active projects, and a high-capacity HDD (like the WD My Passport) for your backups. By separating your "Working" drive from your "Archive" drive, you'll have a faster, safer, and more professional workflow.

## Key Takeaways
*   Edit off SSDs for speed; backup to HDDs for capacity.
*   1,000 MB/s is the "Sweet Spot" for 4K video editing.
*   Thunderbolt drives are required for 8K or high-bitrate ProRes.
*   Always follow the "3-2-1" backup rule (3 copies, 2 different media, 1 offsite).
*   EditorVault helps save drive space by providing on-demand assets.
